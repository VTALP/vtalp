Lesson Sections

What is an Oscilloscope?

It is a laboratory instrument for observing changing electrical signals.

Waveforms

Sine, square, and triangle waves show different signal shapes.

Frequency

Frequency is the number of cycles per second, measured in hertz.

Amplitude

Amplitude is the signal height from the centre line to the peak.

Period

Period is the time for one complete cycle.

Time/Div and Volt/Div

These controls scale the signal horizontally and vertically.

Frequency Explanation

Frequency describes how many waveform cycles occur every second. Higher frequency means more cycles fit across the screen, so the waveform appears closer together horizontally.

Amplitude Explanation

Amplitude describes the signal height from the centre reference line to the peak. Increasing amplitude makes the trace taller on the display.

Time/Div Explanation

Time/Div controls how much time each horizontal grid division represents. Smaller Time/Div values zoom in on fast changes, while larger values show more time across the screen.

Volt/Div Explanation

Volt/Div controls how much voltage each vertical grid division represents. It helps scale the waveform so the trace is visible without being too small or clipped.

Clean Signal vs Noisy Signal

Clean Signal: A smooth and stable trace that is easy to measure.

Noisy Signal: A rough or unstable trace caused by unwanted interference added to the real signal.

Did You Know?

  • A function generator creates test signals so engineers can check circuit behavior safely.
  • PWM signals are commonly used for LED dimming and motor speed control.
  • Audio can be represented as an electrical waveform, which is why oscilloscopes can display sound signals.
  • Electrical noise can affect measurements, communication systems, and sensor readings.

Step-by-Step Usage Guide

  1. Turn on the oscilloscope.
  2. Select a waveform or signal source.
  3. Adjust frequency and observe cycle spacing.
  4. Adjust amplitude and observe signal height.
  5. Read frequency, period, amplitude, and Vpp values.

Common Mistakes

  • Confusing amplitude with peak-to-peak voltage.
  • Ignoring Time/Div when counting waveform cycles.
  • Trying to compare signals without checking the scale.
  • Forgetting to connect a probe before reading the trace.
  • Assuming a signal is absent before checking power and trigger status.

Safety and Best Practice Tips

  • Start with a clear signal and simple waveform.
  • Check Time/Div and Volt/Div before interpreting values.
  • Use trigger status to stabilize repeating signals.
  • Use safe low-voltage signals during beginner practice.
  • Keep probe ground leads connected correctly.
  • Do not probe high-voltage circuits without supervision.
